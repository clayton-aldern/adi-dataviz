Why visualize data?

This is a serious motivating question.

There are worthy alternatives! What about data sonification?

My main suggestion is: We visualize data to make sense of the world.

Sometimes this sensemaking comes in the form of exploratory analysis (making sense for the self).

Sometimes it serves a persuasive, narrative purpose (making sense for others).

Sometimes you get lucky and it does both.

Sensemaking

Who has heard of John Snow?

So: In the case of John Snow, we actually got quite lucky here.


Claim: Intent is a sufficient but not necessary condition for sensemaking.

It is very difficult to optimize for luck, so you should seek to optimize for intentionality.

Intent in data visualization

Recall that charts should further a narrative. They do so by being additive and/or memorable. True in science, journalism, the public sector, etc.

They can also compress a narrative. If it takes 300 words to explain a chart, you probably don't need it. (But if you can replace 300 words with a chart, then you might!)

Charts should be on the page for a reason.

Interactivity

Ask yourself the same questions. Is it additive? If not, is it memorable*? Is it compressing?

Additionally, interactivity should allow for context-specificity (think: subsetting functions).

Again: It should exist for a reason.

*I don't mean: "Is it so bad that you've been traumatized and can't help but remember it?"

Those were the rules you shouldn't break.

What are the other rules?

Minimalism often undergirds effective communication. Ask yourself: What could I remove from this chart while still retaining its core message?

Some examples we just saw include axis-label removal, minimal legends (color-matched subheads), a lack of gridlines, etc.

Smoothing data can be extraordinarily useful, as well. Data visualization is data communication, not data enumeration.

Conclusions?

Data visualization is a narrative act.

So: Follow the rules in service of your narrative. I'm not just saying this because I'm a journalist. Science is persuasive communication, too.

Break the rules in service of emotional resonance and internalization.
